Instruction Sets For Strangers

Tompkins Square Park

XYZ is a series of graphically striking black and white painted cardboard tetris boxes that were grouped in Tompkins Square Park to redirect pedestrian traffic as it flows through the park. The project plays off the redesign of Tompkins Square in the 1930â€™s which intended to separate large mass gatherings and prevent political mobilization. In XYZ, park goers moved the boxes around, created arches, and gathered for play, or alternately felt apprehensive and distracted. In addition, the clusters of boxes visually lured or deterred park-goers, creating new paths, connections, and flows in the park.

Location

From salt marsh to rioting hub, from needle park to gentrified playground, Tompkins Square Park has a robust and highly diverse history that is synonymous and reflective of New York social issues. In our initial visits to the park, we found that the flow of pedestrian traffic to be dictated by a specific end destination rather than by free movement or exploration of the park. Looking into the history of the park, we discovered this flow was intentionally built when it was redesigned by Robert Moses In 1936, following the many labor riots, to divide and manage large crowds from gathering.

Iteration 1

Our first iteration explored how redirecting traffic could unite and encourage interaction rather than division by playing off the redesign of Tompkins Square Park. We partially dammed one bottleneck walkway by placing a series of passageway at will. white carboard boxes within it and observed users navigate through and change the boxes and This interaction asked the audience to slow down, change course, and interact with the piece; in doing so re-orient themselves and their role in the park.

Iteration 2

Following this initial interaction, we wondered how clusters of objects, rather than a damming structure, could encourage longer and more meaningful changes to this flow. Our second iteration applied a basic grid system and seemingly complimentary but randomized schema to explore how pattern recognition might inform interaction. We recreated the boxes to enable a more playful interactive experience rather than a single block-aid formation. By creating an experience that offered structure as well as choice to the user, our interactive experience fundamentally shifted how people moved through and engaged with Tompkins Square Park.
