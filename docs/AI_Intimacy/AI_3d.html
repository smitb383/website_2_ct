3D Research

Aesthetics and Anatomy of the Ear

To continue to investigate AI and intimacy, I explored how machines collect sensory input in relationship to humans. Additionally I aimed to explore the relationship between aesthetics of sensory inputs and human responsiveness to humanoid objects. I 3D modeled and printed an ear and created a circuit that connects a sound sensor to an Arduino microprocessor in order to create an ear that "can hear". In studying the internal processes of sound to computation in humans and computers, I realized that the processes are based on the input - output black-box paradigm that governs not only machine learning but also understanding of the human brain. I therefore separated my external and internal ears with a partially open black box to show the separation in functional and aesthetic perception.

Process

Following my research in natural language processing, I started researching the anatomy of the human ear both aesthetically and as a tool for collecting and interpreting information from the outside world. I started by exploring the aesthetics of the ear in Maya and quickly learned how complex of a shape it is despite how rudimentary ears are in everyday life. Additionally through attempting 4 different ear models, I realized there are countless approaches to design this shape and countless challenges in achieving something that looks convincingly human.

Research

I began investigating human perception of humanoid objects and the uncanny valley, a theory about humanoid aesthetics developed by Masahiro Mori, that states states the closer that humanoid objects get to resembling a human, while the observer is still aware its non-human, elicits a increasingly strong feelings of uncanniness associated with eeriness and uneasiness in observers. I then began to look further into the process of collecting and transforming sound stimuli into electrical pulses in an ear verses a computer. I was amazed at how similar the two processes are and that sound is electrically received and converted to the "brains" of both systems in an extremely similar fashion.

Sound Sensing

My goal was not to program the ear to process auditory information but represent this as something that is aesthetically human in part and can also receive and process sound in the same way as a traditional human ear. This research led me to consider how the internal processing of stimuli v the external appearance of a humanoid sensory-organ would effect humanoid believability.
